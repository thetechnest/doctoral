Set Theory & Algebra

Core mathematical foundations: sets, relations, functions, and algebraic structures like groups, partial orders, lattices and Boolean algebra. These concepts underpin formal reasoning used across CS and research.

Key Topics Explained

1. Sets: Collections of distinct elements. Operations: union (∪), intersection (∩), difference (−), complement, and power set P(S).

2. Relations: A relation R on sets A×B is a subset of the Cartesian product. Properties include reflexive, symmetric, antisymmetric, and transitive.

3. Functions: Mappings from domain to codomain. Injective (one-to-one), surjective (onto) and bijective functions and inverse functions are fundamental.

4. Groups: An algebraic structure (G,·) with closure, associativity, identity, and inverses. Examples: integers under addition, nonzero reals under multiplication.

5. Partial Orders: A relation that is reflexive, antisymmetric and transitive. A poset may have chains, antichains, maximal and minimal elements.

6. Lattices: Partially ordered sets where any two elements have a unique least upper bound (join) and greatest lower bound (meet). Distributive and modular lattices are special types.

7. Boolean Algebra: Algebraic structure with binary operations ∧ (AND), ∨ (OR), and unary ¬ (NOT) satisfying laws like De Morgan, distributivity, and complementation. Core to digital logic.

8. Composition & Equivalence Relations: Composition of relations and functions; equivalence relations partition a set into equivalence classes.

9. Homomorphisms & Isomorphisms: Structure-preserving maps between algebraic structures (e.g., group homomorphism). Isomorphism implies structural equivalence.

10. Applications: Formal methods, database theory (relational algebra), logic design, lattice-based access control, and switching algebra in circuits.
